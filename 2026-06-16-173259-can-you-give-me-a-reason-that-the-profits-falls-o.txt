● Now I have a complete picture of the codebase. Here's the explanation:

  ---
  There are two layered causes, and they stack:

  1. Cross-boundary sample exclusion (structural — always happens)

  In prepare.py:_build_index (lines 348–353), a bar is only kept in the test set
  if both its entry timestamp and its bar_close_time (triple-barrier exit) fall
  within the test fold's window:

  in_exit = in_split(exit_ts, split, val_s, test_s)
  if in_entry and in_exit:
      rows.append((tid, int(p)))
  else:
      n_dropped_cross_boundary += 1  # ← silent drop

  The triple-barrier labeler looks forward width_minutes into the future to find
  a barrier hit. For every bar entered in the last width_minutes of the test 
  period, the exit timestamp falls past test_end_exclusive — so those bars are
  silently dropped from the test set entirely. The equity curve gets zero new
  trades from the final width_minutes of every fold, causing the curve to
  flatline near the end.

  2. Model staleness on the most recent fold (always structural in walk-forward)

  The last walk-forward fold trains on the longest expanding window of
  historical data, then tests on the most recent period — the furthest point in
  time from the bulk of the training distribution. Financial markets are
  non-stationary: patterns that held for 6+ months of training data often weaken
  or reverse in the latest 1-month test window. This pushes the last fold's PnL
  below earlier folds, causing the cumulative curve to drop rather than just
  flatline.

  ---
  Why it happens "every time": Cause #1 is deterministic — it's baked into the
  boundary logic. Cause #2 is near-universal with expanding-window walk-forward
  because the last fold always has the worst train/test temporal gap relative to
  the total span.

  The practical fix for #1 is to let the labeler see a small buffer of data
  beyond test_end_exclusive (keeping the entry-based split boundary strict).
  Cause #2 is harder — it's the fundamental challenge of temporal
  generalization.
