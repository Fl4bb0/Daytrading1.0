‚óŹ Confirmed ‚ÄĒ this matches what the agent found. Here's the answer:

  Yes, but it's a "new entry" cooldown, not a "don't immediately flip" guard ‚ÄĒ 
  and it's off by default.

  - _apply_ticker_cooldown() in src/kvant/evaluation/runner.py:735-760 drops any
  new trade on a ticker that arrives less than ticker_cooldown_minutes after
  the prior entry on that same ticker. This is what you're looking for, but the
  default is ticker_cooldown_minutes=0 ‚ÄĒ i.e. disabled ‚ÄĒ wherever it's wired up
  (lines ~156, 231, 296, 369,¬†500).
  - _apply_action_probability_thresholds() (lines 763-785) demotes
  low-confidence BUY/SHORT predictions to HOLD, which indirectly reduces
  flip-flopping but doesn't track "did we just exit this ticker."
  - The meta-model scoring in src/kvant/meta/features.py:106-159 weights signals
  by historical per-ticker/per-side profitability, which also indirectly
  discourages re-entering a side that's been losing ‚ÄĒ but again it's a soft
  bias, not a hard block.
  - Round-trip transaction fees (runner.py:503,554) are subtracted from PnL, so
  churning costs money in the backtest, but nothing stops the model from doing
  it if it predicts each flip is profitable net of fees.

  So: the mechanism exists (ticker_cooldown_minutes) but isn't active unless you
  explicitly set it in your config. If you want, I can find where you'd pass
  that parameter for your current walk-forward/backtest setup and turn it on.

‚óŹ That confirms the picture ‚ÄĒ there's no drawdown circuit breaker or
  sector/correlation cap anywhere in the core evaluation/execution path. Here's
  the verified answer:

  Yes ‚ÄĒ beyond the immediate-re-entry case (already covered by the cooldown), I
  found four structural reasons losses can cluster at specific tickers or time
  windows:

  1. Meta-score lags regime changes ‚ÄĒ src/kvant/meta/features.py:140-141. The
  per-ticker/side score is (local_sum + shrinkage_k * global_mean) / (local_n + 
  shrinkage_k), built only from resolved past trades. When a ticker flips into a
  new downtrend, its score still reflects the old (profitable) regime until
  enough new losing trades accumulate to drag it down ‚ÄĒ so the model keeps
  buying into the downtrend for a while before the score catches up.
  2. Top-K selection has no diversification constraint ‚ÄĒ runner.py:696-697
  (top_k_per_timestamp). Candidates are ranked purely by meta_score per
  timestamp; if two or three correlated tickers (same sector, same factor
  exposure) all score well at once, all get selected together. A drawdown
  hitting that shared factor hits all selected positions simultaneously instead
  of being diversified away.
  3. No per-ticker or sector exposure cap, and no drawdown circuit breaker ‚ÄĒ
  runner.py:469,526-554. Position sizing is purely 1/n_pools per trade with no
  check on how many pools are already concentrated in one ticker/sector, and
  nothing halts new entries after a string of losses. Confirmed by grep: no
  drawdown, max_concurrent, or sector cap logic exists anywhere in src/kvant.
  4. Fixed/volatility-scaled barrier widths create per-ticker loss signatures ‚ÄĒ
  low-volatility tickers get a relatively tight stop, high-volatility ones a
  wide one, so some tickers structurally bleed small losses repeatedly while
  others structurally take bigger hits, concentrating drawdown contribution
  unevenly.

  One claim from my first pass didn't hold up on closer reading and I'm dropping
  it: _apply_short_execution_policy (runner.py:812-817) just converts
  SHORT‚ÜíHOLD when allow_short=false ‚ÄĒ it does not cause the system to go long on
  downtrending names instead. Long-only mode means downtrend-correct SHORT
  calls get skipped, not converted to buys.

  Most actionable starting point if you want to dig in: add a simple
  per-ticker/sector concentration cap on top_k_per_timestamp selection, since
  that's the one with no mitigating logic at all today. Want me to implement
  that, or look closer at one of the other three first?

‚óŹ This isn't new ‚ÄĒ it's the same two structural causes I traced for you in an
  earlier session, and I just re-verified both are still present in the current
  code (your edits and mine didn't touch this path):

  1. It's partly in the code (deterministic, confirmed at prepare.py:336-353).
  Any bar whose entry falls in the last width_minutes (=40 min, from
  pipeline.toml:23) of a split window gets dropped, because its triple-barrier
  exit lands past the window boundary ‚ÄĒ _build_index silently excludes it
  (n_dropped_cross_boundary). Every split's last 40 minutes of potential trades
  just vanish. This makes the equity curve flatline right before each window
  ends ‚ÄĒ it doesn't actively lose money, it just stops earning for the last
  stretch.

  2. It's partly the dataset's nature, not a config of the dataset. Confirmed in
  utils/walk_forward.py:58-65: in expanding mode, training always starts at
  start_month and grows up to max_train_span_months=6, then tests on the next
  month out. The last fold in any walk-forward run is always the one furthest in
  time from the bulk of its training data ‚ÄĒ by construction. Markets are
  non-stationary, so whatever the model learned from Jan‚ÄďMay often doesn't hold
  as well in the newest month it's tested on. This isn't corrupted data, it's
  that "the most recent reality" is the one piece of data the model has never
  adapted to.

  3. Run length (test_span_months=1) is a multiplier, not the root cause. A
  1-month test fold has maybe ~21 trading days after skip_opening_minutes. The
  40-minute dead-zone from #1 eats a much bigger slice of a 1-month fold's
  trading time than it would of a 3-month fold, and with fewer trades to average
  over, a rough last few days reads as "the whole fold falling apart" even if
  it's partly small-sample noise. Lengthening test_span_months would dilute both
  effects but wouldn't fix #2.

  Practical takeaway: #1 is fully fixable ‚ÄĒ let the labeler see a small buffer
  of bars past test_end_exclusive (without moving the entry-based split
  boundary), so the last 40 minutes of each fold stop getting dropped for free.
  #2 is the hard one ‚ÄĒ it's the actual cost of testing on the newest, least-seen
  month every single time, and no labeling fix removes it.
