‚óŹ What allow_short = true does mechanically: it stops
  _apply_short_execution_policy() from demoting SHORT predictions to HOLD
  (evaluation/runner.py:812-817). SHORT predictions then flow into the same
  pool-allocation logic as BUY (evaluation/runner.py:524-554): each trade claims
  1/n_pools (currently 10) of capital from entry to exit, and PnL is
  trade_pnl_pct / n_pools ‚ÄĒ identical mechanics for both directions.

  That symmetry is the problem for your live-funds question. The backtest treats
  long and short as economically identical, but they aren't:

  - Margin, not cash, funds a short. Buying $X of stock costs $X. Shorting $X of
  stock under US Reg T requires roughly 150% of position value in margin (you
  need a margin account, not a cash account). With a fixed pool of capital, your
  short capacity per pool isn't the same as your long capacity ‚ÄĒ the backtest's
  "1/n_pools either way" assumption overstates how much short exposure your
  real funds can actually carry.
  - Borrow availability isn't modeled. Some of your 99 tickers may be
  hard-to-borrow or unavailable to short at a given moment (or carry a borrow
  fee). The backtest assumes every SHORT signal is instantly fillable; live,
  some will be rejected or cost extra.
  - Asymmetric risk. A long position's max loss is -100%. A short's max loss is
  theoretically unbounded (price can run past your stop before it fills). The
  backtest's symmetric pool-PnL doesn't capture that tail risk.
  - PDT rule, if this is a <$25k US account: this strategy day-trades on
  1-minute bars across many tickers, so you'd hit the pattern-day-trader
  threshold (4+ round trips in 5 days) fast, regardless of long/short mix ‚ÄĒ
  separate issue, but worth knowing.

  So: flipping the flag is the right first step to see whether the model's SHORT
  signal is even any good (you can't evaluate signal quality while it's being
  silently discarded). But the resulting equity curve will be optimistic 
  relative to live trading with a small/fixed account, because it assumes shorts
  are exactly as capital-efficient as longs.

  Want me to just flip the flag and rerun for now (to evaluate signal quality),
  or also adjust the pool simulation to apply a margin multiplier on SHORT
  trades so the backtest numbers are closer to what fixed real funds could
  achieve?
